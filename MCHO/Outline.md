Part 3: Interactive Activities Design
Activity 1: "Revolution Comparison Chart"
Placement: After Segment 2 Duration: 3 minutes Type: Interactive matching game
Learning Objective: Identify parallels between Industrial Revolution and AI Revolution
Activity Description: Interactive chart where students match historical issues with modern equivalents:
•	Child labor in factories → Data privacy violations in youth apps
•	Unsafe working conditions → Algorithmic discrimination
•	Wealth inequality → Digital divide
•	Factory owners controlling wages → Tech companies controlling algorithms
Student Task: Drag-and-drop to make connections, then write one sentence explaining the parallel they found most surprising.
________________________________________
Activity 2: "What Would YOU Do?"
Placement: After Segment 3 Duration: 5 minutes Type: Ethical dilemma scenarios
Learning Objective: Apply ethical thinking to real situations teens face
Scenario 1: School Surveillance Your school wants to use AI to monitor student social media for mental health risks. The system flags posts containing words like "depressed," "can't do this anymore," or "give up." Your friend's vent post gets flagged and she's called to the counselor.
•	Questions: Is this protecting students or invading privacy? How would you redesign this system ethically?
Scenario 2: College AI Screening You applied to your dream college. Later you learn their AI rejected 60% of applications before any human reviewed them, and the AI seems to favor certain zip codes and school districts over others.
•	Questions: What principle is violated? What should change?
Scenario 3: Infinite Scroll Algorithm Your favorite social media app's AI keeps you scrolling for 3+ hours by perfectly predicting what you want to see next. You know it's designed to be addictive.
•	Questions: Is this ethical? Who's responsible? What would "human dignity" look like here?
Student Task: Choose one scenario, select a response option, explain reasoning using module vocabulary
________________________________________
Activity 3: "Stakeholder Perspectives"
Placement: Between Segments 5 and 6 Duration: 5 minutes Type: Perspective-taking reading + reflection
Learning Objective: Understand diverse viewpoints on AI ethics
Perspectives presented (short 2-paragraph profiles):
Maya - Gig Worker (Age 32) "I deliver food using an app. The algorithm decides which orders I get and rates my performance. Last week my rating dropped because of late deliveries during a snowstorm. Now I get fewer high-paying orders. I have no way to appeal to a human."
Jordan - Tech CEO (Age 45) "I run an AI startup. We're trying to balance ethics with competition. If we slow down for ethics reviews, our competitors will beat us to market. Investors expect rapid growth. I believe in doing right, but the system pressures us to move fast."
Alex - High School Junior (Age 16) "I use ChatGPT to help me understand difficult concepts and brainstorm essay ideas. But I've seen classmates copy-paste entire essays. Teachers are cracking down on AI use, but it's honestly so helpful when I'm stuck. Where's the line?"
Ms. Rodriguez - English Teacher (Age 38) "I can usually tell when students use AI to write their essays. The voice changes, it sounds too polished. But I'm not sure how to prove it. I want students to learn, but I also don't want to ban a tool that could help them. This is exhausting."
Student Task:
1.	Read all four perspectives
2.	Reflection: "Which perspective surprised me most and why?"
3.	Application: "How would I balance these competing needs?"

Question 3: True or False with explanation:
"The Common Good principle means AI should benefit the majority of people."
Answer: FALSE
Explanation: The Common Good means AI should allow EVERYONE to thrive, not just most people. It's about ensuring technology benefits all members of society, especially marginalized groups, not just the majority.
