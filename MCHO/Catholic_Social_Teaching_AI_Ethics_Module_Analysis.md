# Catholic Social Teaching & AI Ethics Module Analysis
## Comprehensive Review and Design Recommendations

**Date:** October 27, 2025
**Source:** MCHO/Project 11.txt (7-minute video transcript)
**Target Audience:** High school students (ages 14-18)
**Reviewers:** AI Literacy Content Reviewer + Teen Content Reviewer

---

## Executive Summary

This ~7-minute video presents Catholic Social Teaching principles (Human Dignity, Common Good, Solidarity) as an ethical framework for AI development, drawing parallels between the Industrial Revolution and the current AI revolution. The content has **strong pedagogical value**, particularly the historical parallel and the Rome Call for AI Ethics partnership, but requires **significant modifications** to meet educational standards for technical accuracy, inclusivity, and student engagement.

### Key Strengths ✅
- Powerful "industrialization of thought" concept
- Relevant Industrial Revolution parallels
- Concrete examples (loan rejections, algorithmic bias)
- Rome Call partnership (Vatican + tech companies) is fascinating
- Appropriate length and conversational tone

### Critical Issues ⚠️
- **Anthropomorphization:** AI described as "thinking" and "deciding"
- **Religious exclusivity:** Heavy Catholic framing without acknowledging other perspectives
- **Engagement challenges:** 7 minutes of lecture without interactive breaks
- **Lack of teen-relevant examples:** Needs scenarios from students' actual lives
- **No concrete actions:** Students learn ethics is important but not what they can DO

## Part 1: Educational Content Analysis

### 1. AGE-APPROPRIATENESS ASSESSMENT

#### Vocabulary Issues Requiring Revision

| **Term/Phrase** | **Issue** | **Suggested Fix** |
|-----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| "Industrialization of thought" | Abstract without definition | "Just as factories automated physical labor, AI automates mental tasks like writing, analyzing, and creating" |
| "Cognitive labor" | Academic jargon | "Mental work" or "thinking tasks" |
| "Algorithmic bias" | Undefined technical term | Add: "when AI systems make unfair decisions based on flawed training data" |
| "Rerum novarum" | Latin without context | "Rerum Novarum (Latin for 'Of New Things') - a document about workers' rights" |
| "The common good" | Vague philosophy | "Ensuring technology benefits everyone in society, not just a wealthy few" |

#### Anthropomorphization Problems (CRITICAL)

**Line 118-120:** "You can't be rejected for a loan or a job by a machine without a human being in the loop"
- ❌ **Problem:** Suggests machines make decisions
- ✅ **Fix:** "AI systems that evaluate loan or job applications must have human oversight and final decision-making"

**Line 454-455:** "how efficient some algorithm thinks you are"
- ❌ **Problem:** Algorithms don't "think"
- ✅ **Fix:** "how an algorithm evaluates or scores your data"

#### Religious Framing Concerns

The content heavily centers Catholic perspectives without acknowledging diversity:

**Recommendation:** Add inclusive framing
> "This module explores Catholic Social Teaching as one approach to AI ethics. Many other religious and secular frameworks offer valuable perspectives. Students of all backgrounds are encouraged to consider how their own values might guide ethical AI use."

### 2. VIDEO SEGMENTATION PLAN

Breaking the 7-minute video into digestible 1-2 minute segments:

#### Segment 1: "Standing at the Edge of a New World" (0:00-1:30)
- **Content:** AI as massive technological shift, need for ethical compass
- **Key quote:** "We are living through a new industrial revolution"
- **Transition point:** After establishing scale of change, before diving into parallels

#### Segment 2: "From Factory Floors to Digital Minds" (1:30-3:00)
- **Content:** Industrial Revolution vs. AI Revolution comparison
- **Key concepts:**
  - Physical labor automation → Cognitive labor automation
  - Machine operation skills → Prompt engineering skills
  - Factory safety concerns → Algorithmic bias concerns
- **Transition point:** After establishing parallel, before introducing solution

#### Segment 3: "Ancient Wisdom for Modern Problems" (3:00-4:00)
- **Content:** Pope Leo VII's Rerum Novarum response to Industrial Revolution
- **Key concepts:** Historical precedent for ethical frameworks during tech disruption
- **Transition point:** After establishing historical response, before explaining principles

#### Segment 4: "Human Dignity, Common Good, and Solidarity" (4:00-5:30)
- **Content:** Detailed explanation of three principles
- **Key concepts:**
  - Human Dignity: Worth beyond productivity/data
  - Common Good: Innovation benefits everyone
  - Solidarity: Collective responsibility
- **Transition point:** After principle explanation, before real-world application

#### Segment 5: "From Philosophy to Practice" (5:30-6:45)
- **Content:** Rome Call for AI Ethics (Vatican + Microsoft + IBM)
- **Key concepts:** Transparency, accountability, human oversight
- **Transition point:** After Rome Call, before final challenge

#### Segment 6: "Wisdom Over Speed" (6:45-7:30)
- **Content:** The choice between ethical development vs. rapid profit
- **Key concepts:** Personal courage, following ethical principles
- **Closing question:** "Do we have the courage to follow where the compass is pointing?"

## Part 3: Interactive Activities Design

### Activity 1: "Revolution Comparison Chart"
**Placement:** After Segment 2
**Duration:** 3 minutes
**Type:** Interactive matching game

**Learning Objective:** Identify parallels between Industrial Revolution and AI Revolution

**Activity Description:**
Interactive chart where students match historical issues with modern equivalents:
- Child labor in factories → Data privacy violations in youth apps
- Unsafe working conditions → Algorithmic discrimination
- Wealth inequality → Digital divide
- Factory owners controlling wages → Tech companies controlling algorithms

**Student Task:** Drag-and-drop to make connections, then write one sentence explaining the parallel they found most surprising.

---

### Activity 2: "What Would YOU Do?"
**Placement:** After Segment 3
**Duration:** 5 minutes
**Type:** Ethical dilemma scenarios

**Learning Objective:** Apply ethical thinking to real situations teens face

**Scenario 1: School Surveillance**
Your school wants to use AI to monitor student social media for mental health risks. The system flags posts containing words like "depressed," "can't do this anymore," or "give up." Your friend's vent post gets flagged and she's called to the counselor.
- **Questions:** Is this protecting students or invading privacy? How would you redesign this system ethically?

**Scenario 2: College AI Screening**
You applied to your dream college. Later you learn their AI rejected 60% of applications before any human reviewed them, and the AI seems to favor certain zip codes and school districts over others.
- **Questions:** What principle is violated? What should change?

**Scenario 3: Infinite Scroll Algorithm**
Your favorite social media app's AI keeps you scrolling for 3+ hours by perfectly predicting what you want to see next. You know it's designed to be addictive.
- **Questions:** Is this ethical? Who's responsible? What would "human dignity" look like here?

**Student Task:** Choose one scenario, select a response option, explain reasoning using module vocabulary

### Activity 4: "Design Your Own AI Ethics Rule"
**Placement:** After Segment 5
**Duration:** 4 minutes
**Type:** Creative rule-writing

**Learning Objective:** Create practical ethics guidelines

**Template:**
"To protect [principle], AI systems that [specific function] must [specific requirement]."

**Examples:**
- "To protect human dignity, AI systems that evaluate job applications must allow humans to review and override all decisions."
- "To promote the common good, AI tutoring tools must be available free to students regardless of family income."
- "To practice solidarity, tech companies must report and fix biased algorithms when they're discovered."

**Student Task:** Write one specific rule they would implement if they were at the Rome Call table. Must use at least one principle and be specific (not vague).

---

### Activity 5: "Personal AI Audit"
**Placement:** After Segment 6
**Duration:** 3 minutes
**Type:** Self-reflection checklist

**Learning Objective:** Evaluate personal relationship with AI

**Checklist:**
- [ ] I know when I'm interacting with AI vs. humans online
- [ ] I understand how apps collect and use my data
- [ ] I fact-check AI-generated content before trusting it
- [ ] I use AI as a tool to learn, not to complete work for me
- [ ] I've thought about how AI algorithms influence what I see
- [ ] I would recognize AI-generated images or deepfakes
- [ ] I could explain one ethical concern about AI to a friend

**Student Task:**
1. Check all that apply
2. Identify ONE area to improve
3. Write specific action plan (e.g., "This week I'll turn on data privacy settings in my top 3 apps")

### Activity 6: "Stakeholder Perspectives"
**Placement:** Between Segments 5 and 6
**Duration:** 5 minutes
**Type:** Perspective-taking reading + reflection

**Learning Objective:** Understand diverse viewpoints on AI ethics

**Perspectives presented (short 2-paragraph profiles):**

**Maya - Gig Worker (Age 32)**
"I deliver food using an app. The algorithm decides which orders I get and rates my performance. Last week my rating dropped because of late deliveries during a snowstorm. Now I get fewer high-paying orders. I have no way to appeal to a human."

**Jordan - Tech CEO (Age 45)**
"I run an AI startup. We're trying to balance ethics with competition. If we slow down for ethics reviews, our competitors will beat us to market. Investors expect rapid growth. I believe in doing right, but the system pressures us to move fast."

**Alex - High School Junior (Age 16)**
"I use ChatGPT to help me understand difficult concepts and brainstorm essay ideas. But I've seen classmates copy-paste entire essays. Teachers are cracking down on AI use, but it's honestly so helpful when I'm stuck. Where's the line?"

**Ms. Rodriguez - English Teacher (Age 38)**
"I can usually tell when students use AI to write their essays. The voice changes, it sounds too polished. But I'm not sure how to prove it. I want students to learn, but I also don't want to ban a tool that could help them. This is exhausting."

**Student Task:**
1. Read all four perspectives
2. Answer: How might each person view the three principles differently?
3. Reflection: "Which perspective surprised me most and why?"
4. Application: "How would I balance these competing needs?"

### Quiz 2: Three Principles Check
**Placement:** After Segment 4
**Duration:** 3 minutes
**Format:** Short answer + matching

**Question 1:** In your own words, explain what "Human Dignity" means in the context of AI. (2-3 sentences)

**Model answer:** Every person has value regardless of how productive they are or what an algorithm calculates about them. Your worth isn't determined by AI scores or data points. AI systems must respect human value and not reduce people to just numbers.

---

**Question 2:** Match the principle to the example:

| Example | Principle |
|---------|-----------|
| Ensuring rural students have equal access to AI education tools | \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ |
| Requiring human oversight for AI hiring decisions | \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ |
| Everyone working together to fix biased algorithms | \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ |

**Answers:** Common Good, Human Dignity, Solidarity

---

**Question 3:** True or False with explanation:

"The Common Good principle means AI should benefit the majority of people."

**Answer:** FALSE

**Explanation:** The Common Good means AI should allow EVERYONE to thrive, not just most people. It's about ensuring technology benefits all members of society, especially marginalized groups, not just the majority.

---

**Question 4:** Scenario Application:

A company creates an AI hiring tool that saves time by automatically rejecting candidates. The system rejects more women than men for engineering jobs. Which principle(s) is this violating? Explain your answer.

**Model answer:** This violates both Human Dignity (reducing candidates to data points without human review) and The Common Good (creating inequality rather than opportunity for all). It may also violate Solidarity if the company doesn't take collective responsibility to fix the bias.

## Part 6: Critical Modifications Required

### 1. Remove All Anthropomorphization

**Before → After:**

| ❌ Original Language | ✅ Corrected Language |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| "algorithm thinks you are" | "algorithm evaluates your data" |
| "rejected by a machine" | "rejected by an AI system without human review" |
| "AI decides" | "AI system outputs a decision that humans must review" |
| "AI understands" | "AI processes patterns in data" |
| "AI wants" | "AI is programmed to" |

**Rationale:** Maintaining technical accuracy is crucial for educational integrity. Students must understand AI as a tool, not a thinking entity.

---

### 2. Add Inclusive Religious Framing

**Opening Disclaimer (Required):**
> "This module explores Catholic Social Teaching as one approach to AI ethics. These principles—human dignity, common good, and solidarity—appear in many ethical and religious traditions worldwide. Students of all backgrounds are encouraged to consider how their own values, beliefs, and cultural perspectives might guide ethical AI use. You are not required to agree with this framework; the goal is to practice ethical reasoning about technology."

**Throughout Module:**
- Use phrases like "this framework suggests" rather than "ethics requires"
- Acknowledge: "Many other religious and philosophical traditions offer valuable perspectives on AI ethics"
- Provide alternative exit ticket for students uncomfortable with religious framing

---

### 3. Add Academic Integrity Statements

**Required at Module Start:**
> "⚠️ **Important Note About AI and Schoolwork:** This module teaches you to understand AI as a learning tool. Using AI to complete assignments, essays, or homework for you violates academic integrity policies. AI can help you understand difficult concepts, brainstorm ideas, or check your thinking—but your work must be your own."

**Reinforce During Activities:**
- Activity examples should show AI helping with understanding, not completion
- Exit ticket should ask students to reflect on ethical AI use for learning
- Personal AI Audit should include "I use AI to learn, not to complete work for me"

---

### 4. Preserve and Enhance Student Agency

**Agency-Diminishing Language → Empowering Language:**

| ❌ Passive/Inevitable | ✅ Active/Choice-Based |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| "we're all racing headfirst into this AI driven future" | "As AI tools become more common, we have choices about how to use them" |
| "AI will transform society" | "We collectively shape how AI transforms society" |
| "AI creates problems" | "Our choices about AI systems create problems we must address together" |
| "AI is taking over" | "We're choosing to integrate AI in certain ways" |

**Add Explicit Agency Statements:**
- "You choose when and how to use AI tools"
- "You evaluate whether AI output is accurate and appropriate"
- "You remain responsible for your work and decisions"
- "You have power to demand ethical AI development"

---

### 5. Connect to Other Modules

**Cross-Module Vocabulary Alignment:**

| This Module | Connected Module | Link Point |
|------------|------------------|------------|
| "Algorithmic bias" | LLM Limitations | "Remember from LLM Limitations: AI trained on biased data produces biased outputs" |
| "Transparency in AI systems" | Understanding LLMs | "As we learned in Understanding LLMs, these systems are prediction machines—we must understand their limitations" |
| "Data collection concerns" | Privacy & Data Rights | "The Privacy module explored how your data is collected—here we ask if that collection respects human dignity" |
| "Environmental cost of AI" | AI Environmental Impact | "Solidarity means addressing AI's environmental impact together" |

**Recommended Module Placement:**
This module works best **after** Understanding LLMs and LLM Limitations, but **before** Privacy & Data Rights, so students have technical foundation before ethical frameworks.

## Part 9: Assessment Rubric

### How to Evaluate Student Learning

#### Exit Ticket Rubric (AI-Assisted Grading)

**4 - Exceeds Expectations**
- Clearly connects principle to specific, personal example
- Shows deep understanding of how ethics translates to design
- Considers multiple perspectives or trade-offs
- Proposes concrete, feasible changes
- Writing is clear, organized, and thoughtful

**3 - Meets Expectations**
- Connects principle to relevant example
- Shows understanding of core concept
- Explains reasoning with supporting details
- Identifies at least one specific change
- Writing is clear and coherent

**2 - Approaching Expectations**
- Makes general connection but lacks specificity
- Shows partial understanding of concept
- Reasoning is vague or underdeveloped
- Suggestions are too broad or unrealistic
- Writing needs more clarity

**1 - Below Expectations**
- Connection to principle is unclear or missing
- Misunderstands core concept
- Little or no reasoning provided
- No specific suggestions
- Writing is unclear or off-topic

**0 - Incomplete**
- Did not respond or response is nonsensical
- Less than 50 words (pre-filter catches this)

---

#### Activity Participation

**Formative Assessment (not graded):**
- Activity 1-7 completion tracked
- Teacher can see which activities students engaged with
- Focus on participation, not "correct" answers
- Use data to identify students who may need support

## Part 11: Risk Assessment & Mitigation

### Identified Risks

#### Risk 1: Religious Controversy
**Likelihood:** Medium
**Impact:** High

**Mitigation:**
- Clear inclusive disclaimer at start
- Alternative exit ticket prompt
- Frame as "one approach among many"
- Acknowledge diverse student backgrounds
- Provide teacher guidance for handling questions

---

#### Risk 2: Student Disengagement
**Likelihood:** Medium (if not interactive)
**Impact:** High (learning outcomes suffer)

**Mitigation:**
- Break video into 1-2 min segments
- Activity every 2-3 minutes
- Teen-relevant examples throughout
- Concrete actions students can take
- Optional deeper engagement paths

---

#### Risk 3: Overly Philosophical Content
**Likelihood:** High (based on teen feedback)
**Impact:** Medium (students tune out but may still pass)

**Mitigation:**
- Lead with scenarios, follow with philosophy
- Define all abstract terms immediately
- Connect every principle to personal examples
- Use modern language, avoid academic jargon
- Include "translate to teen" explanations

---

#### Risk 4: Academic Integrity Concerns
**Likelihood:** Medium
**Impact:** Very High (if students misapply lessons)

**Mitigation:**
- Explicit statements about AI and homework
- Reinforce in activities
- Include in Personal AI Audit
- Teacher guidance document
- Exit ticket addresses ethical AI use for learning

---

#### Risk 5: Technical Accuracy Issues
**Likelihood:** Low (with corrections)
**Impact:** Critical (undermines other modules)

**Mitigation:**
- Remove all anthropomorphic language
- Align vocabulary with existing modules
- Technical review before deployment
- Consistent definitions throughout
- Link to Understanding LLMs for technical foundation
