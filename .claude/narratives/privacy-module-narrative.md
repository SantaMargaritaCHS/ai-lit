## THE HOOK: Jordan's Story

Picture this. It's October, and Jordan—a 17-year-old senior—is stressed about college applications. The essay needs to be perfect. So Jordan opens up a popular AI chatbot and asks for help.

"Hey, can you help me revise my college essay? It's about overcoming personal challenges."

The AI responds enthusiastically. Jordan pastes the whole thing in:

> "My name is Jordan Chen, and I'm a senior at Lincoln High, Class of 2027. It hasn't been easy maintaining my 3.7 GPA. My anxiety has been a constant battle, especially since my parents' divorce last year..."

The AI gives great feedback. Jordan feels good about the essay. Session over.

**Two weeks later.**

A completely different student—Alex, from a different school—logs into the same AI tool. Alex asks for help brainstorming a college essay topic.

And the AI responds:

> "Certainly. Many compelling essays focus on resilience. For example, I recently worked with Jordan Chen from Lincoln High, Class of 2027, who wrote a powerful essay about navigating anxiety and their parents' divorce while maintaining a 3.7 GPA..."

Wait. WHAT?

Jordan's name. Jordan's school. Jordan's mental health struggles. Jordan's family situation. All of it—just casually shared with a complete stranger.

This isn't a made-up horror story. This is how many AI tools actually work.

---

## THE PROBLEM: How AI Remembers Everything

So how did that happen? Here's the thing most people don't understand:

Many consumer AI tools are designed to **learn from every conversation** you have with them. It's not a bug—it's the business model.

Think of it like this:

1. **You tell the AI something.** You share your thoughts, your ideas, your personal information.

2. **The AI "learns" it.** Your conversation gets saved and used to train the AI model. It becomes part of the AI's vast memory.

3. **It repeats it to someone else.** Weeks or months later, the AI might use your details as a "good example" for another user—without realizing it's sensitive or private.

Here's the scary part: Your data doesn't just sit in a database somewhere. It gets **woven into the AI's core programming**. This makes it nearly impossible to remove later. What you share can become a permanent part of the model.

The AI doesn't understand privacy. It doesn't understand trust. It doesn't understand that your college essay is different from a Wikipedia article. To the AI, it's all just data.

## THE FINE PRINT: Terms & Conditions Reality Check

"But wait," you might be thinking. "Don't they have to tell us they're doing this?"

Yes. They do. It's all in the Terms and Conditions—that super long, boring document you click "I Agree" on without reading.

Be honest: How long do you actually spend reading those terms?

Here's a fun fact: The average person would need **45 minutes** just to read Snapchat's Terms of Service. Not understand it—just read the words. And that's just ONE app.

Nobody reads these things. The companies know that. They're counting on it.

So while you're clicking "I Agree" in 3 seconds flat, you're actually agreeing to things like:

- "We may use your conversations to train our AI models"
- "Your content may be shared with third parties for advertising purposes"
- "You grant us a perpetual, royalty-free license to use your content"

That last one? That means **forever**. They own it. Forever.

## THE QUIZ: What You Think You Know vs. Reality

Here's where it gets interesting. Most of what people believe about AI privacy is actually wrong.

**True or False: ChatGPT Free uses your conversations to train its AI models.**

The answer is TRUE. When you use the free version of ChatGPT, your conversations help train OpenAI's models. You have to actively opt-out in settings to prevent this. Most people don't know that setting exists.

**True or False: Snapchat My AI chats disappear automatically like regular Snaps.**

FALSE. This is a big one. Unlike regular Snaps that disappear, your My AI conversations are stored **forever** until you manually delete them. Snap treats AI chats completely differently from friend chats.

**True or False: Character.AI can share your conversations with third parties for advertising.**

TRUE. Character.AI's privacy policy allows them to share your data with third parties for targeted advertising. They claim it's not technically "selling" for money, but the effect is the same.

**True or False: X/Grok can use your AI conversations forever with no way to take it back.**

TRUE. The January 2026 terms update grants X a "perpetual, royalty-free license" to use everything you type to Grok—forever. This includes training AI models with your conversations.

But here's some good news:

**True or False: Microsoft Copilot Education uses student conversations to train its AI.**

FALSE. School versions of Microsoft Copilot do NOT use your conversations for training. Your school has a legal agreement that protects student data. This is why school-provided AI tools are safer.

**True or False: Claude.ai trains on your conversations by default.**

FALSE. Unlike most AI chatbots, Claude does NOT train on your conversations by default. You have to actively opt-in if you want to share your data.

The pattern is clear: **Not all AI tools are created equal.**

## THE COMPARISON: School-Safe vs. Consumer Tools

Let's break down the key differences between the AI tools your school provides and the ones you download yourself.

**School-Safe Tools** (like Microsoft Copilot Education, SchoolAI, Snorkl):
- Your conversations are NOT used for AI training
- NO personalized ads based on your chats
- Data protected under legal agreements (FERPA compliance)
- Schools can delete your data
- Built specifically for students with privacy as the default

**Consumer Tools** (like ChatGPT Free, Snapchat My AI, Character.AI, Grok):
- Conversations often used to train AI models
- May be used for personalized advertising
- You have to actively find and enable privacy settings
- Data can be retained indefinitely
- Some grant "perpetual licenses" to your content

The difference comes down to one question: **Who is the customer?**

With school tools, your school is the customer. Microsoft, Google, and others have signed legal contracts promising to protect student data.

With consumer tools, **you are the product**. These companies make money by using your data—either to train better AI models (which they sell) or to show you targeted ads.

Some tools are riskier than others. Here are the ones that should raise red flags:

## YOUR ACTION PLAN: Three Steps to Protect Yourself

You don't have to stop using AI. You just need to be smart about it.

**Step 1: Know Your Tools**

For schoolwork and anything involving personal information, always use the AI tools provided by your school—like Microsoft Copilot Education, SchoolAI, or Snorkl.

For casual curiosity on consumer AI tools, treat them like a public forum. Don't share secrets, personal struggles, or identifying details.

**Step 2: Anonymize Everything**

When using a consumer AI tool for help with a story or essay, strip out all personal details:
- Your name: "Jordan Chen" → "Student A"
- Your school: "Lincoln High" → "a high school"
- Personal details: "my parents' divorce" → "a difficult family situation"
- Specifics: "my 3.7 GPA" → "a good GPA"

**Step 3: Check and Delete Your History**

Most consumer AI tools have settings to view and delete your conversation history. Get in the habit of clearing it regularly.

Look for privacy settings that let you opt out of having your conversations used for model training. It might not be the default, so you have to actively find it and turn it on.

## KNOW YOUR RIGHTS: Laws That Protect You

You're not powerless. There are actual laws designed to protect your privacy.

**COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act)**
If you're under 13, companies need parental consent to collect your data. That's why most AI tools require you to be 13+. Companies that violate COPPA face millions in fines.

**State Privacy Laws (California, Virginia, and more)**
States like California (CCPA) give you the right to know what data companies collect, request deletion, and opt out of data sales. More states are passing similar laws every year.

**EU AI Act & GDPR (If You're in Europe)**
European laws are even stronger. The GDPR gives EU residents formal opt-out rights from AI training, and the new AI Act requires transparency about how AI systems use your data.

## THE GOLDEN RULE

Here's the one thing to remember from this entire module:

> **"If you wouldn't post it on a public billboard with your name on it, don't type it into a consumer AI tool."**

That's it. That's the rule.

AI can be incredibly helpful—for homework, creative projects, problem-solving. But you have to treat consumer AI tools like a public conversation that advertisers, trainers, and strangers might read.

The good news? **You are in control.**

- You have the choice to use school-safe tools that protect your data by default.
- You have the choice to anonymize your information on consumer platforms.
- You have the choice to read beyond the "I Agree" button and understand what you're signing up for.

Knowledge is power. And now you have it.
