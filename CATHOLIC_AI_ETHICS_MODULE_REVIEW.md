# Catholic Social Teaching & AI Ethics Module Review
## Educational Content Analysis for Ages 14-18

---

## 1. AGE-APPROPRIATENESS REVIEW

### **Strengths:**
- Overall vocabulary is accessible and conversational
- Good use of everyday examples (loans, jobs, social media)
- Relatable metaphors (compass, rearview mirror, playbook)
- Appropriate length (~7 minutes) for teen attention spans

### **CRITICAL ISSUES** (must be fixed):

**Anthropomorphization Problems:**
- [Line 118-120] "You can't be rejected for a loan or a job by a machine without a human being in the loop" → Problem: Suggests machine makes decisions → Suggested fix: "AI systems that evaluate loan or job applications must have human oversight and final decision-making"

- [Line 454-455] "how efficient some algorithm thinks you are" → Problem: Algorithms don't "think" → Suggested fix: "how an algorithm evaluates or scores your data"

- [Line 831-833] "The yardstick we use for AI can't just be, hey, is it useful or is it efficient?" → Problem: Too casual, lacks technical precision → Suggested fix: "The criteria for evaluating AI systems shouldn't only be functionality or efficiency"

### **VOCABULARY CONCERNS:**

- **"Industrialization of thought"** → Issue: Abstract concept needs clearer definition → Suggestion: "Just as factories automated physical labor, AI automates mental tasks like writing, analyzing, and creating - this is the 'industrialization of thought'"

- **"Cognitive labor"** → Issue: Academic jargon → Suggestion: "Mental work" or "thinking tasks"

- **"Algorithmic bias"** → Issue: Undefined technical term → Suggestion: Add definition: "when AI systems make unfair decisions based on flawed training data"

- **"Rerum novarum"** → Issue: Latin term without sufficient context → Suggestion: "Rerum Novarum (Latin for 'Of New Things') - a historic document about workers' rights during the Industrial Revolution"

- **"The common good"** → Issue: Vague philosophical concept → Suggestion: "The common good means ensuring technology benefits everyone in society, not just a wealthy few"

### **Religious Framing Concerns:**

- The content heavily centers Catholic perspectives without acknowledging other ethical frameworks
- Suggestion: Frame as "one important ethical framework among several" and acknowledge diverse perspectives
- Add disclaimer: "While this module explores Catholic Social Teaching as one approach to AI ethics, many other religious and secular frameworks offer valuable perspectives"

### **Cross-Module Vocabulary Alignment Needed:**

Terms that should match other AI literacy modules:
- Use "AI system" or "AI tool" consistently (not "machine")
- Use "training data" when discussing bias
- Use "Large Language Model (LLM)" when relevant
- Use "algorithm" consistently with technical definition from other modules

## 2. VIDEO SEGMENTATION PLAN

### **Segment 1: The AI Revolution** (0:00 - 1:30)
**Title:** "Standing at the Edge of a New World"
**Description:** Introduction to AI as a new industrial revolution transforming how we think and create.
**Key concepts:** Scale of change, need for ethical guidance, parallels to 19th century

### **Segment 2: Industrialization of Thought** (1:30 - 3:00)
**Title:** "From Factory Floors to Digital Minds"
**Description:** Comparing the first Industrial Revolution's automation of physical labor to AI's automation of mental tasks.
**Key concepts:** Cognitive automation, prompt engineering vs. machine operation, new ethical challenges

### **Segment 3: Learning from History** (3:00 - 4:00)
**Title:** "Ancient Wisdom for Modern Problems"
**Description:** How 19th-century responses to industrialization (Rerum Novarum) can guide us through AI challenges.
**Key concepts:** Catholic Social Teaching origins, finding middle ground between extremes

### **Segment 4: Three Core Principles** (4:00 - 5:30)
**Title:** "Human Dignity, Common Good, and Solidarity"
**Description:** Detailed explanation of the three foundational principles for ethical AI development.
**Key concepts:** Individual worth, technology for all, collective responsibility

### **Segment 5: The Rome Call** (5:30 - 6:45)
**Title:** "From Philosophy to Practice"
**Description:** How Vatican and tech companies partnered to create practical AI ethics guidelines.
**Key concepts:** Real-world application, transparency, accountability, human oversight

### **Segment 6: Your Choice Matters** (6:45 - 7:30)
**Title:** "Wisdom Over Speed"
**Description:** The challenge of choosing ethical development over rapid deployment and profit.
**Key concepts:** Personal responsibility, courage to follow ethical principles

## 3. INTERACTIVE ACTIVITIES

### **Activity 1: Revolution Comparison Chart**
**Placement:** After Segment 2
**Title:** "Two Revolutions, Similar Challenges"
**Learning Objective:** Students identify parallels between industrial and AI revolutions
**Description:** Interactive chart where students match Industrial Revolution issues (child labor, unsafe conditions, wealth inequality) with modern AI equivalents (data privacy violations, algorithmic discrimination, digital divide). Students drag and drop to make connections, then write one sentence explaining a parallel they found most interesting.

### **Activity 2: Ethical Dilemma Scenarios**
**Placement:** After Segment 3
**Title:** "What Would You Do?"
**Learning Objective:** Apply ethical thinking to real AI situations teens face
**Description:** Present 3 scenarios:
1. Your school wants to use AI to monitor student social media for mental health risks
2. A college uses AI to screen applications, but it seems to favor certain zip codes
3. Your favorite app's AI recommends content that keeps you scrolling for hours

Students choose how to respond and explain their reasoning using vocabulary from the module.

### **Activity 3: Principle Application Exercise**
**Placement:** After Segment 4
**Title:** "Principles in Practice"
**Learning Objective:** Connect abstract principles to concrete teen experiences
**Description:** Students match real AI features to principles:
- Instagram's "take a break" reminders → Human Dignity or Common Good?
- TikTok's algorithm showing diverse creators → Common Good or Solidarity?
- YouTube's human review of flagged content → Human Dignity or Solidarity?

Students explain their choices in 2-3 sentences.

### **Activity 4: Design Your Own AI Ethics Rule**
**Placement:** After Segment 5
**Title:** "If You Were at the Rome Call Table"
**Learning Objective:** Students create practical ethics guidelines
**Description:** Students write one specific rule for AI companies following this template:
"To protect [principle], AI systems that [specific function] must [specific requirement]."
Example: "To protect human dignity, AI systems that evaluate job applications must allow humans to review and override all decisions."

### **Activity 5: Personal AI Audit**
**Placement:** After Segment 6
**Title:** "Check Your Own AI Use"
**Learning Objective:** Students evaluate their personal relationship with AI
**Description:** Checklist activity where students assess their AI interactions:
- Do I know when I'm interacting with AI vs. humans?
- Do I understand how apps use my data?
- Do I fact-check AI-generated content?
- Do I use AI as a tool or let it make decisions for me?

Students identify one area to improve and write a specific action plan.

### **Activity 6: Stakeholder Perspectives**
**Placement:** Between Segments 5 and 6
**Title:** "Walking in Different Shoes"
**Learning Objective:** Understand diverse viewpoints on AI ethics
**Description:** Students read short perspectives from:
- A gig worker whose job is threatened by AI
- A tech CEO developing new AI products
- A student using AI for homework help
- A teacher trying to detect AI-generated work

Students identify how each person might view the three principles differently and write about whose perspective surprised them most.

### **Activity 7: Future Vision Board**
**Placement:** As final activity before exit ticket
**Title:** "The AI World You Want to Live In"
**Learning Objective:** Students envision ethical AI implementation
**Description:** Digital vision board where students select 5 features they want in their AI-powered future from options like:
- "AI tutors that help me understand, not complete work for me"
- "Social media that promotes real connections"
- "AI that helps doctors but doesn't replace human care"
- "Technology that reduces inequality instead of increasing it"

Students write a paragraph about their top priority and why.

## 4. COMPREHENSION CHECKS

### **Quiz 1: Understanding the Parallel** (After Segment 2)
**Multiple Choice Questions:**

1. What does "industrialization of thought" mean?
   a) Robots taking over factories
   b) AI automating mental and creative tasks [CORRECT]
   c) People thinking like machines
   d) Factories producing computers

2. How is learning prompt engineering similar to learning to operate factory machines in the 1800s?
   a) Both require years of training
   b) Both are physical skills
   c) Both are new skills needed to work with new technology [CORRECT]
   d) Both are being replaced by AI

3. What ethical concern has shifted from "factory safety" to modern AI?
   a) Worker wages
   b) Algorithmic bias [CORRECT]
   c) Child labor
   d) Working hours

### **Quiz 2: Three Principles Check** (After Segment 4)
**Short Answer Questions:**

1. **Human Dignity means:** (Students type 1-2 sentences)
   Model answer: "Every person has value regardless of productivity or what an algorithm calculates about them."

2. **Match the principle to the example:**
   - Ensuring rural students have equal access to AI education tools → [Common Good]
   - Requiring human oversight for AI hiring decisions → [Human Dignity]
   - Everyone working together to fix biased algorithms → [Solidarity]

3. **True/False with explanation:**
   "The Common Good principle means AI should only benefit the majority of people."
   FALSE - Explain: The Common Good means AI should allow EVERYONE to thrive, not just most people.

### **Quiz 3: Rome Call Understanding** (After Segment 5)
**Application Questions:**

1. Why is it significant that tech companies partnered with the Vatican?
   Multiple choice:
   a) The Vatican controls technology development
   b) It shows different sectors can collaborate on ethics [CORRECT]
   c) Tech companies are religious organizations
   d) The Vatican owns AI technology

2. How does "transparency" in AI connect to the Common Good?
   Short answer prompt: "When we understand how AI makes decisions, we can..."
   (Students complete in 2-3 sentences)

## 5. EXIT TICKET DESIGN

### **Reflection Prompts:**

**Prompt 1: Personal Application**
"Think about the AI tools you use daily (social media algorithms, homework helpers, gaming AI, recommendation systems). Choose one and explain how applying ONE of the three principles (Human Dignity, Common Good, or Solidarity) could make that tool better for teen users. Be specific about what would change and why it matters to you."

**Prompt 2: Critical Analysis**
"The video suggests we need 'courage' to choose ethical AI over speed and profit. What pressures do you think make it hard for companies to slow down and consider ethics? What could motivate them to change? Consider both business and social factors in your response."

**Prompt 3: Future Responsibility**
"You're part of the generation that will shape how AI develops. Based on what you learned about Catholic Social Teaching's approach to the Industrial Revolution, what lesson from history do you think is most important for your generation to remember as AI becomes more powerful? Explain how you personally might apply this lesson."

**Alternative Prompt (for students uncomfortable with religious framing):**
"The three principles discussed (human dignity, common good, solidarity) exist in many ethical frameworks, not just Catholic teaching. Choose a different source of ethical guidance you respect (could be another religion, philosophy, or secular framework). How might that framework approach AI ethics? What similar or different principles might it emphasize?"

## 6. EDUCATIONAL PHILOSOPHY ALIGNMENT

### **Strengths:**
- ✅ Emphasizes critical thinking about AI's role in society
- ✅ Positions students as future decision-makers
- ✅ Encourages evaluation of AI tools rather than blind acceptance
- ✅ Focuses on ethical use rather than technical manipulation

### **CRITICAL ISSUES** (must be fixed):

**Agency-Diminishing Language:**
- Video says "we're all racing headfirst into this AI driven future" → Problem: Suggests inevitability without choice → Better framing: "As AI tools become more common, we have choices about how to use them"

**Anthropomorphization:**
- Multiple instances of AI "thinking," "deciding," or "rejecting" → Must be reframed as "AI systems process," "AI tools evaluate," "AI programs output"

**Homework Completion Risk:**
- No explicit mention that AI shouldn't complete schoolwork → Must add clear statement: "While AI can help you understand concepts and learn new skills, using it to complete assignments for you undermines your learning and violates academic integrity"

### **Recommendations for Student Agency:**

1. **Add explicit agency statements:**
   - "You choose when and how to use AI tools"
   - "You evaluate whether AI output is accurate and appropriate"
   - "You remain responsible for your work and decisions"

2. **Reframe passive descriptions:**
   - Instead of "AI will transform society" → "We collectively shape how AI transforms society"
   - Instead of "AI creates problems" → "Our use of AI systems can create problems we must address"

3. **Include empowerment activities:**
   - Have students write their own ethical guidelines
   - Let students evaluate real AI tools against ethical principles
   - Include "opt-out" discussions - when NOT to use AI

## 7. CROSS-MODULE NOTES

### **Terminology to Standardize:**

1. **Use across all modules:**
   - "AI system" or "AI tool" (not "AI" as an entity)
   - "Training data" when discussing bias
   - "Algorithm" (with consistent definition)
   - "Human oversight" (not "human in the loop")
   - "AI-generated content" (not "AI creations")

2. **Avoid across all modules:**
   - AI "thinking," "wanting," "trying," "understanding"
   - AI as "partner," "teammate," "collaborator"
   - "Artificial intelligence" as conscious entity
   - Suggestions that AI "helps with homework" (use "helps understand concepts")

3. **Connect to other modules:**
   - Link "algorithmic bias" discussion to LLM Limitations module
   - Connect "transparency" to Understanding LLMs module
   - Reference Privacy & Data Rights when discussing data collection
   - Tie environmental impact of AI development to AI Environmental Impact module

### **Conceptual Bridges:**

- This module's emphasis on human oversight directly supports Understanding LLMs' message about human verification
- The "industrialization of thought" concept provides historical context for Introduction to Gen AI
- Solidarity principle connects to collective action in AI Environmental Impact module
- Human dignity principle reinforces Privacy & Data Rights concepts

## 8. IMPLEMENTATION RECOMMENDATIONS

### **Module Structure:**
1. Opening hook: Personal AI use inventory (2 min)
2. Video Segment 1 + Discussion (2 min)
3. Activity 1: Revolution Comparison (3 min)
4. Video Segment 2 + Quick check (2 min)
5. Activity 2: Ethical Dilemmas (5 min)
6. Video Segment 3 + Quiz 1 (3 min)
7. Video Segment 4 + Activity 3 (4 min)
8. Quiz 2: Three Principles (2 min)
9. Video Segment 5 + Activity 4 (4 min)
10. Video Segment 6 + Activity 5 (3 min)
11. Exit Ticket with AI feedback (5 min)
12. Certificate generation (1 min)

**Total estimated time: 35-40 minutes**

### **Critical Safeguards:**

1. **Add opening disclaimer:**
   "This module explores one ethical framework for AI. Students of all backgrounds are encouraged to consider how their own values and beliefs might guide ethical AI use."

2. **Include academic integrity reminder:**
   "Remember: Using AI to complete schoolwork for you violates academic integrity. This module teaches you to use AI as a learning tool, not a substitute for your own work."

3. **Emphasize critical evaluation:**
   Every activity should reinforce that students must critically evaluate AI systems, not simply accept them.

### **Accessibility Considerations:**

- Provide transcript for all video segments
- Ensure all interactive elements are keyboard navigable
- Use clear contrast ratios (minimum 4.5:1)
- Include alt text for any visual elements
- Offer text alternatives for drag-and-drop activities

## FINAL ASSESSMENT

This content has strong potential for adaptation but requires significant modifications to:
1. Remove anthropomorphic language completely
2. Explicitly prohibit academic dishonesty
3. Frame religious content inclusively
4. Preserve and enhance student agency
5. Align vocabulary with existing AI literacy modules

The historical parallel between industrial and AI revolutions is pedagogically powerful and should be preserved as the module's core strength. The three principles provide a useful framework that transcends religious boundaries when properly contextualized.

With these modifications, this could become a valuable addition to the AI literacy curriculum, offering students a historically-grounded, ethically-focused perspective on AI development and use.
