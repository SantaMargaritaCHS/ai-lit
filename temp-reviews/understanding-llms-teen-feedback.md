# Understanding LLMs Module - Teen Review by Maya (15, Sophomore)

**Date:** October 18, 2024
**Reviewer:** Maya (15 y/o, B/C student who actually wants to help make this better)

---

## First Impression

Okay so when I first saw this module I was like "Understanding Large Language Models" sounds super technical and boring, ngl. But then I saw it starts with a "magic trick" and I was kinda curious? Like it's trying to grab my attention which is good. The purple/blue gradient background is pretty but also feels a bit... educational video-y if that makes sense? Like something they'd show us in computer class.

The 17 phases thing made me go "ugh that's a LOT" but then I saw most videos are under a minute which is actually perfect. I hate when educational videos drag on forever.

## What Worked

**The "Beat the Predictor" Game (Phase 5)**
This was actually pretty cool! I put "gecko" as my favorite animal (my brother has one) and seeing the AI predict boring stuff like "dog" and "cat" made me feel kinda smart? Like I outsmarted it. The message about having "agency and creativity" vs the AI just having "statistics" actually hit different. Made me think about how I'm unique and the AI is just... math.

**Short Video Clips**
THANK YOU for keeping videos under 90 seconds mostly! The 22-second "magic trick" one is perfect length. I can actually pay attention for that long without checking my phone. The longest one is only 1:27 which is totally doable.

**The Dial Simulation (Phase 11)**
This was lowkey fascinating watching the percentages change. The visual of the dials going up and down made it SO much clearer than if someone just explained it with words. Like I actually get how it "learns" now - it's just adjusting numbers, not actually thinking. The animation was smooth and kept me watching.

**Visual Design of Activities**
The polls and quizzes look clean, not like those crusty Google Forms teachers usually use. The emoji usage (✨🧠🔢) helps break up the text and makes it feel less formal.

**The Tokenization Demo**
Even though I didn't see the full code, having something interactive where I can type my own text and see how it breaks down is way better than just being told about it. Hands-on stuff >>> lectures.

## What Didn't Work

**The Welcome Phase**
Having a whole separate "welcome" phase feels unnecessary? Like just start with the magic trick video to grab attention immediately. I'd probably be tempted to just click through the welcome without reading.

**Too Many Quizzes Back-to-Back**
Phases 7, 9, and 15 are all quizzes. By the third one I'd be like "another quiz??" Maybe mix in a different type of activity? Or combine some of them? It starts feeling like busywork even if the content is different.

**Some Language Still Feels Textbook-y**
Phrases like "statistically common in the data it was trained on" and "neural network as pattern-finding web" still sound like they're from a textbook. Can't you just say "it picks common stuff" and "the computer network finds patterns"?

**The Exit Ticket Requirements**
100 characters minimum AND 15 words? And it won't let me continue if I don't write enough? That's gonna annoy people SO much. I get you want thoughtful answers but forcing us to write a certain amount just makes us pad our answers with fluff. "Monitored for inappropriate language" also feels kinda surveillance-y and weird.

**"You're in Control" Message Repetition**
Okay I get it, I'm in control, the AI isn't magic. But this point gets hammered like 5+ times. After the third time I was like "yeah I GET it already." Maybe vary the message more?

## What I Actually Learned

Honestly? More than I expected. Here's what's actually gonna stick with me:

1. **AI is just a really fancy autocorrect.** Like when my phone suggests the next word when I'm texting, but way more complex. It's not actually "smart" or thinking about what it's saying.

2. **The training loop thing is just trial and error with numbers.** It guesses, checks if it's right, adjusts the percentages. That's literally it. No magic, no thinking, just math doing math things billions of times.

3. **When ChatGPT gives me homework help, it's not "knowing" the answer - it's predicting what words usually come after my question.** This actually makes me trust it less for important stuff, which is probably good?

4. **I have creativity and can surprise the AI, but it can only work with patterns it's seen.** The "Beat the Predictor" game really drove this home.

What's weird is I'll probably think about this next time I use ChatGPT. Like I'll actually remember it's just predicting patterns, not understanding my question.

## How to Make It Better

### Immediate Fixes:

1. **Combine or Cut Quizzes:** Three quizzes is too many. Maybe combine the "Context Matters" and "What's in the Web" into one activity? Or make one of them a different format like a drag-and-drop matching game.

2. **Chill on the Exit Ticket Requirements:** Make it like 50 characters or 10 words. If someone writes a thoughtful short answer, that should be enough. The AI feedback is cool but the strict requirements are gonna make people hate it.

3. **Start with Action:** Skip the welcome and jump straight to the magic trick video. Hook us IMMEDIATELY.

4. **More Relatable Examples:** Instead of "The cat sat on the mat," use examples we actually encounter. Like "When you type 'how do I' in Google, it suggests..." or "When you text 'omw' your phone suggests 'home' because..."

5. **Simplify the Technical Language:**
   - "Pattern-finding web" → "Pattern detector"
   - "Statistically likely" → "Most common"
   - "Neural network" → "Computer network" (or just avoid the term)

### Bigger Improvements:

1. **Add a "Create Your Own" Activity:** Let us make our own "predictor" with simple choices. Like we pick 5 responses for "My weekend was ___" and then see how a basic predictor would work. Make it OURS.

2. **Real-World Scenarios:** Add examples of when this matters:
   - "Your friend used ChatGPT for their essay and got caught because..."
   - "Someone believed everything an AI told them about health and..."
   - "A student used AI for math homework but didn't check the work and..."

3. **Make it Social:** Add a class poll feature where we can see what animals everyone chose in Beat the Predictor. Or what percentage of the class got fooled by the "magic." We love seeing what others picked.

4. **Gamify the Whole Thing:** Give points for completing activities, maybe unlock fun facts or memes about AI fails. We're used to apps that reward us for everything.

5. **Add AI Fail Compilation:** Show us funny/weird examples of AI getting things wrong. Like those screenshots where ChatGPT says something completely ridiculous. We'd actually pay attention to that and it reinforces that AI makes mistakes.

### What's Missing:

- **Why should I care right now?** How does this help me TODAY, not just "someday"? Connect it to things like:
  - Not getting scammed by fake AI-generated content
  - Using ChatGPT better for homework (ethically)
  - Understanding why my teacher can tell when I use AI

- **The "So What?"** After learning all this, what do I DO differently? Give us concrete actions like "Three things to always check when using AI for homework"

- **Peer Stories:** Real quotes from other students about times AI helped or failed them. We trust other teens more than adults telling us stuff.

---

## Final Thoughts

This module is actually way better than most educational content we get. It's trying to be engaging and mostly succeeds. The core concept - that AI is just a pattern predictor, not magic - comes through clearly. The interactive elements are good when they're not overdone.

But it still feels a bit like it's trying too hard to be "fun" while also being educational. Just be real with us. We can handle the truth about AI without all the gamification if you explain WHY it matters to our actual lives.

The best part is the "Beat the Predictor" game because it made ME feel smart and creative compared to the AI. More of that energy - making us feel empowered rather than lectured to - would make this module genuinely great instead of just "pretty good for school stuff."

Would I recommend this to a friend? Honestly, only if they were struggling with understanding AI for a class. But that's already better than 90% of educational content which I'd never recommend to anyone ever.

**Grade I'd Give This Module:** B+
(Good effort, mostly engaging, but still feels like "educational content" rather than something I'd choose to learn)
